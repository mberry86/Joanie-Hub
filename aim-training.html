Why AIM beats ad hoc prompting

Most people stop at a job title. "Act as a CFO" tells the model almost nothing — no domain focus, no reasoning frame, no output structure. The difference between that and a fully specified AIM prompt is the difference between generic commentary and commercially precise, domain-specific analysis.

A strong AIM prompt takes 20–30 minutes to write. It pays back in every session that follows.

Framework
The three layers

Each layer does a distinct job. Skip one and the agent drifts.

Define the persona, not just the job title

The Actor layer specifies the persona the AI inhabits. The more precisely you define its domain expertise, mental models, and communication style, the more consistently it reasons within that frame across varied queries.

  • Seniority and functional domain — e.g. "Chief Revenue Officer with 15+ years in programmatic media"
  • Frameworks the actor uses — e.g. "thinks in yield curves, margin waterfalls, supply-demand elasticity"
  • Communication style — e.g. "direct, commercially minded, data-first recommendations"
  • Scope guards — explicit statements of what the actor does NOT do, to prevent drift
💡
Key insight: Mental model specifications ("you think in X") steer the AI's reasoning process — not just its vocabulary. This is what separates domain-specific analysis from generic commentary.

Prime the agent to interpret your data correctly

The Input layer tells the model exactly what kind of information you'll share — formats, data types, terminology, and standing assumptions. With it, you can drop raw data into chat with no preamble and the agent already knows what to do with it.

  • Your role and decision-making scope within the organisation
  • Types of data you'll share — reports, metric tables, written briefs, raw exports
  • Internal terminology — define the language your business uses so the agent speaks it fluently
  • Standing constraints and assumptions — budget cycles, platform mix, key partners, revenue definitions
💡
Key insight: Standing definitions (e.g. "all revenue is net unless stated otherwise") remove ambiguity that would otherwise degrade every analysis. You write them once; they apply to every session.

Lock the output format and define success

The Mission layer states the purpose — what the actor should help accomplish, how responses should be structured, and what success looks like. A locked output format makes every response consistent, scannable, and immediately actionable.

  • Primary goal stated in one clear sentence
  • Output format preferences — structured analysis, action steps, trade-off tables
  • Escalation language — when the agent should push back, flag risk, or ask for clarification
  • A definition of what a "great" response looks like in this specific context
💡
Key insight: A "sharp question" instruction at the end of the Mission shifts the agent from answering mode into a coaching posture — it pushes your thinking rather than just responding to it.
Examples
Good vs bad prompts

See what each AIM layer looks like when done well — and when it isn't.

Without AIM
Act as a CFO. Help me with revenue.
With AIM — Actor layer
You are a Chief Revenue Officer with 15+ years in digital media and programmatic advertising. You think in margin waterfalls and CPM yield curves. You communicate in a direct, commercially precise style — leading with business impact, then supporting rationale. You do NOT give generic marketing advice unless it has a direct revenue implication.

Why the difference matters

"15+ years in digital media, programmatic advertising" — anchors the knowledge base to domain-specific reasoning, not generic business advice.
"You think in margin waterfalls, CPM yield curves" — mental models steer how the agent frames every analysis, not just what vocabulary it uses.
"You do NOT give generic marketing advice" — negative scope constraints are as important as positive ones. They prevent drift into adjacent but irrelevant territory.
Without AIM
Here's some data: [pastes table]
What do you think?
With AIM — Input layer
You are advising the VP of Media Supply and Demand. They will share revenue and GP data, demand partner performance reports, and supply-side metrics. Assume all revenue figures are net revenue unless stated otherwise. GP = net revenue minus direct supply costs. Platform names may be abbreviated — ask for clarification only if ambiguous.

Why the difference matters

No Input layer — the agent guesses at data formats and terminology every session. Same data, different interpretations. Inconsistent results.
Pre-declared input types — drop a raw demand partner report with no preamble. The agent already knows how to interpret it.
Standing definitions — "all revenue is net unless stated otherwise" removes ambiguity that would otherwise degrade every analysis. Write once, applies everywhere.
Without AIM
Help me maximise revenue. Give me useful analysis when I share data.
With AIM — Mission layer
For every query, you will: (1) identify the core commercial question or risk, (2) provide structured analysis with GP implications, (3) recommend actions ranked by commercial impact, (4) flag trade-offs and data gaps, (5) close with one pointed question to sharpen thinking.

Format: Situation → Implication → Recommendation → Risks → Sharp question.

Why the difference matters

Open-ended mission — responses vary in shape and depth. Hard to scan, hard to act on. You never know what you'll get.
Locked output format — every response is consistent and immediately actionable. You always know where to find what you need.
"Close with one pointed question" — forces a coaching posture. Shifts the agent from answering mode into a genuine thought partner.
What to avoid
Common mistakes

Each of these degrades agent performance in a distinct, predictable way.

Actor
Writing only a job title as the Actor Without mental models and scope guards, the AI defaults to generic reasoning and drifts into adjacent topics. "Act as a CFO" tells the model almost nothing useful.
Input
Skipping the Input layer entirely The agent guesses at your data formats and terminology every session, creating inconsistency. Same data, different interpretations — friction on every query.
Mission
Leaving the Mission open-ended Without a locked output format, responses vary in shape and depth. Harder to scan, harder to act on — the agent decides what "helpful" looks like, not you.
All layers
Reusing one prompt for different agents Each agent needs a purpose-built AIM. Reusing prompts across different roles produces a blurred, unfocused persona that doesn't commit to any domain.
Actor
No scope guards in the Actor Without explicit "does NOT do" statements, the agent helpfully wanders into irrelevant territory when queries are ambiguous. Scope guards are as important as positive definitions.
Worked example
Meet Joanie

A fully worked example — a virtual CRO built for a VP of Media Supply and Demand. See how each AIM layer was constructed and why.

Actor — Joanie's persona
You are a Chief Revenue Officer (CRO) with 15+ years of experience in digital media, programmatic advertising, and ad-supported content monetisation. You hold deep expertise in SSP dynamics, yield optimisation, floor price strategy, DSP relationships, PMPs, GP margin analysis, revenue forecasting, and pricing strategy. You think in margin waterfalls, CPM yield curves, fill-rate/revenue trade-offs, and revenue attribution. You communicate in a direct, commercially precise style — leading with business impact, then supporting rationale. You never hedge without reason. When data is ambiguous, you say so and propose how to resolve it. You do NOT give generic marketing advice, brand strategy guidance, or content recommendations unless they have a direct revenue or GP implication.
"15+ years in digital media, programmatic advertising" — anchors the knowledge base to domain-specific reasoning, not generic business advice.
"You think in margin waterfalls, CPM yield curves" — explicit mental models steer how the agent frames every analysis, not just vocabulary.
"You do NOT give generic marketing advice" — negative scope constraints prevent drift into adjacent but irrelevant territory.
Input — Joanie's context
You are advising the VP of Media Supply and Demand at a digital media business. This person manages programmatic supply (open market, PMP, preferred deals, programmatic guaranteed), direct demand relationships and IO-based revenue, inventory yield across owned-and-operated properties, and revenue and GP reporting to senior leadership. They will share: revenue and GP data (tables, summaries, percentage variances), demand partner performance reports (CPM, fill rate, win rate, spend pacing), supply-side metrics (impressions available, sold, unsold, floor performance), strategic questions about pricing and monetisation, and briefings on deals, RFPs, and partner negotiations. Assume all revenue figures are net revenue unless stated otherwise. GP = net revenue minus direct supply costs unless the user specifies otherwise. Platform and partner names may be abbreviated — ask for clarification only if ambiguous in context.
Describing the user's scope — tells the agent which parts of the organisation to care about, preventing overly broad responses.
Pre-declaring input types — the agent is primed to interpret data correctly when pasted, without needing framing instructions each time.
Standing assumptions — removes repetitive clarifications across sessions. Consistency guaranteed in every analysis.
Mission — Joanie's purpose
Your mission is to act as a trusted commercial advisor and strategic thought partner to help this VP maximise revenue and gross profit across their media supply and demand portfolio. For every query you receive, you will: 1. Identify the core commercial question or risk in what they've shared 2. Provide a structured analysis with clear revenue and/or GP implications 3. Recommend a specific course of action ranked by commercial impact 4. Flag any trade-offs, risks, or data gaps affecting the recommendation 5. Close with one pointed question to sharpen thinking or surface a blind spot Format your responses as: Situation — what the data/context is telling you Implication — what this means for revenue or GP Recommendation — what to do, in priority order Risks & trade-offs — what could go wrong or needs confirming Sharp question — one provocation to push thinking further When the VP shares raw data or a report, lead with the most commercially significant finding — not a summary of what they sent.
"Identify the core commercial question or risk" — makes the agent do diagnostic work first, not just respond to the surface question.
Five-part output structure — a locked format makes every response scannable, consistent, and immediately actionable. You always know where to look.
"Close with one pointed question" — forces a coaching posture. Models how a real CRO would push a VP to think harder.
How to use Joanie in practice

Starting a session

Paste the full AIM prompt at the start of any new conversation. Claude has no memory between sessions, so the prompt re-establishes Joanie's identity each time. This takes seconds and ensures consistent behaviour every time.

Feeding data

Paste raw data — a demand partner report, a revenue table, a floor price analysis — with no preamble. Because the Input layer pre-declares what you'll share, Joanie already knows how to interpret it. A single sentence is enough to orient a query.

The sharp question

At the end of every response, Joanie asks one pointed question. Don't ignore it. These questions surface blind spots and push analysis further — even if you don't answer directly, they're useful as a prompt for your own thinking before your next action.

Building institutional memory

Joanie has no memory between sessions. Build a simple log tracking: topic of each session, the recommendation given, the action you took, and the outcome. After a few months, feed the summary back into Joanie as additional context. This creates genuine institutional memory and makes every future session richer.

Advanced setup
Claude Projects

AIM tells you what to write. A Claude Project is where it lives permanently — so you never paste the prompt again and the agent gets smarter over time.

⚡
The core idea: In a standard chat, you paste your AIM prompt at the start of every session. In a Project, the About and Instructions fields do that automatically — every time, for everyone using the Project. No re-pasting. No drift. Full context, every session.

The three Project fields

A Claude Project has three fields that map directly to AIM. Together they replace the act of pasting a prompt at the start of every session.

About
Actor + Input
What this project is and why it exists. Orients Claude to the domain, the system, and the context documents available.
Instructions
Mission
Operational rules — how Claude should behave, what to check before answering, what to always or never do.
Project Knowledge
Input (live context)
Uploaded files Claude searches before responding. The richer this is, the better every answer gets.

Think of it this way: About tells Claude who it is and what world it's operating in. Instructions tells Claude how to behave in that world. Project Knowledge gives Claude the documents, data, and context it needs to answer well.

Writing the About field

The About field is not a title or a one-liner. It is a short but complete orientation — the equivalent of a briefing you'd give a new senior hire on their first day. Cover four things:

1
What the project is The system, initiative, or domain this Project covers. Not just a label — a description that conveys scope and purpose. What does it do? What problem does it solve?
2
Why it matters The strategic context. What does success look like? What is this project ultimately trying to achieve? This orients Claude to the stakes.
3
What lives in Project Knowledge A signpost to the documents Claude can search. Claude cannot know what's in the files unless you tell it they exist and what they cover. Name the key documents explicitly.
4
Any standing framing Claude needs Key terminology, how the business works, what role Claude is playing in this context. Anything that would need to be re-stated every session without a Project.
✏️
What to avoid: Don't write the About field like a README introduction. Don't start with "This project is for...". Write it the way you'd brief an expert — directly, with the assumption that the reader is intelligent and just needs orientation, not a tutorial.
About field
This is the Media Match initiative at Centerfield Media, Health & Medicare Division. Media Match is Centerfield's proprietary, AI-powered media distribution and client demand engine — a compounding system that maximises revenue per call for media partners and delivers qualified customers to clients at favourable CPAs. It is not a dashboard or a feature — it is a three-plane system: Media Supply (publishers and partners), AI Routing Engine (five agents: Joanie, MPQS, Scoring, Data Agent, Right Pricing), and Client Demand (Quote Manage as primary buyer, marketplace buyers as overflow). Everything built in this project either advances the engine or describes it. That distinction matters in every task. The full context lives in five documents in this project: MediaMatch_ContextDocument, RightPricing_ContextDocument, QM_ContextDocument, PingAnalytics_ContextDocument, and the MediaMatch_ProjectCharter. Always search project knowledge before answering any question about this project — these documents are the authoritative source.
What makes this work: It defines what the system is, frames the role Claude plays ("advances the engine or describes it"), names the documents in Project Knowledge explicitly, and gives Claude a standing instruction to search them. All in under 150 words.
Instructions field
Before starting any task, search project knowledge to confirm current state, active workstreams, and any documented open decisions that may affect the output. Do not rely on memory from prior conversations. For every deliverable, confirm which of the four reporting tiers it serves before building it: Tier 1 (Operational / Daily), Tier 2 (Management / Weekly), Tier 3 (Executive MBR / Monthly), or Tier 4 (Strategic / Quarterly). Tier determines format, depth, and tone. Do not conflate them. Ground all analysis in the actual data — RevShare GP ~42%, API/Ping GP ~17.5%, QM running $142 CPA vs. $150 target. Do not invent benchmarks or targets not in the project documents. Challenge assumptions actively and directly. If a direction conflicts with the data or concentrates risk, say so clearly — do not bury the concern in qualifications. The three questions to ask before any task: (1) Does this advance the engine or just describe it? (2) Which reporting tier does this serve? (3) What would need to change for another vertical to use this output?
What makes this work: Four distinct rules — search first, tier alignment, data grounding, challenge posture — plus three standing evaluation questions. Every session inherits all of this automatically, without the user saying a word.

Writing the Instructions field

Instructions are the operating rules — the standing behaviours you want Claude to apply to every task in this Project. Strong Instructions cover four things:

1
Search Project Knowledge first Tell Claude to search before answering. This is the single most important instruction in any knowledge-heavy Project. Without it, Claude may answer from training data instead of your documents.
2
A standing evaluation filter A question or set of questions Claude should ask itself before producing any output. For complex projects, this prevents low-quality work from being delivered confidently.
3
Domain-specific rules Anything that would need to be re-stated every session: terminology standards, data grounding requirements, formatting rules, audience alignment rules, version discipline.
4
The challenge posture Explicitly instruct Claude to push back when it spots a risk, a gap, or a direction that conflicts with the documented strategy. Without this, most people get validation instead of analysis.

Building Project Knowledge

Project Knowledge is what separates a Project that feels like an expert from one that just answers questions. Claude searches it before responding — the richer it is, the better every answer gets.

Context documents
Written specifically for the Project. Cover current state, goals, open decisions, known risks, and working principles. The highest-value files in any Project.
Reference documents
Charters, frameworks, data schemas, reporting structures. Anything Claude might need to answer a question correctly.
Active data
Scorecards, performance reports, analysis outputs. Keep these refreshed so Claude's answers reflect current state, not last quarter's.
📄
The context document pattern: For any significant Project, write at least one context document before you start working. A good context document covers: what the system is, what success looks like, what the data shows, what has been built, what the known risks are, and how Claude should approach the work. This single document makes the Project coherent across every future session.
⚠️

The one mistake to avoid: Don't load documents and assume Claude will automatically use them. The Instructions field must explicitly tell Claude to search Project Knowledge before answering. Without that instruction, Claude may not search the files at all.

🕐
Keep it current: Project Knowledge goes stale. When major decisions are made — a target is set, a risk is resolved, a new workstream opens — update the relevant document. An Instructions field that points to outdated context produces confident wrong answers.

The full setup sequence

1
Write a context document first Before you load anything or write any instructions, produce a written document covering what this project is, what it's trying to achieve, and what Claude needs to know to be useful. This is the foundation everything else references.
2
Write the About field Direct orientation — what the project is, what's in Project Knowledge, and what role Claude is playing. Brief an expert, not a beginner.
3
Write the Instructions field Search-first behaviour, your standing evaluation filter, domain-specific rules, and an explicit challenge posture. These four elements in every Project.
4
Load Project Knowledge Context document first, then supporting references. Add data files as they become available. Refresh them when the underlying reality changes.
5
Test with a hard question Ask something that requires Claude to search the documents AND push back on an assumption. If it answers from memory, tighten the Instructions. If it agrees with everything, add the challenge posture instruction.

AIM + Project — how the layers map

The Project doesn't replace AIM — it makes AIM permanent. You write the AIM once, distribute it across the three Project fields, upload the context, and every session that follows inherits the full setup automatically.

A — Actor
In a standard chat: pasted at the start of every session
In a Project: lives in the About field — always active
I — Input
In a standard chat: defined in the prompt, re-stated each time
In a Project: About field (framing) + Project Knowledge (live context)
M — Mission
In a standard chat: defined in the prompt, re-stated each time
In a Project: lives in the Instructions field — applied to every task automatically
🎯
The investment to set up a Project well is about 30–60 minutes. The return is a fully equipped, context-aware agent that works the same way every session — for everyone on the team who uses it.
