Why AIM beats ad hoc prompting

Most people stop at a job title. "Act as a CFO" tells the model almost nothing — no domain focus, no reasoning frame, no output structure. The difference between that and a fully specified AIM prompt is the difference between generic commentary and commercially precise, domain-specific analysis.

A strong AIM prompt takes 20–30 minutes to write. It pays back in every session that follows.

The three layers

Each layer does a distinct job. Skip one and the agent drifts.

A Actor — who the AI becomes

Define the persona, not just the job title

The Actor layer specifies the persona the AI inhabits. The more precisely you define its domain expertise, mental models, and communication style, the more consistently it reasons within that frame across varied queries.

  • Seniority and functional domain — e.g. "Chief Revenue Officer with 15+ years in programmatic media"
  • Frameworks the actor uses — e.g. "thinks in yield curves, margin waterfalls, supply-demand elasticity"
  • Communication style — e.g. "direct, commercially minded, data-first recommendations"
  • Scope guards — explicit statements of what the actor does NOT do, to prevent drift

Key insight: Mental model specifications ("you think in X") steer the AI's reasoning process — not just its vocabulary. This is what separates domain-specific analysis from generic commentary.

I Input — what context you'll provide

Prime the agent to interpret your data correctly

The Input layer tells the model exactly what kind of information you'll share — formats, data types, terminology, and standing assumptions. With it, you can drop raw data into chat with no preamble and the agent already knows what to do with it.

  • Your role and decision-making scope within the organisation
  • Types of data you'll share — reports, metric tables, written briefs, raw exports
  • Internal terminology — define the language your business uses so the agent speaks it fluently
  • Standing constraints and assumptions — budget cycles, platform mix, key partners, revenue definitions

Key insight: Standing definitions (e.g. "all revenue is net unless stated otherwise") remove ambiguity that would otherwise degrade every analysis. You write them once; they apply to every session.

M Mission — the outcome to achieve

Lock the output format and define success

The Mission layer states the purpose — what the actor should help accomplish, how responses should be structured, and what success looks like. A locked output format makes every response consistent, scannable, and immediately actionable.

  • Primary goal stated in one clear sentence
  • Output format preferences — structured analysis, action steps, trade-off tables
  • Escalation language — when the agent should push back, flag risk, or ask for clarification
  • A definition of what a "great" response looks like in this specific context

Key insight: A "sharp question" instruction at the end of the Mission shifts the agent from answering mode into a coaching posture — it pushes your thinking rather than just responding to it.

Good vs bad prompts

See what each AIM layer looks like when done well — and when it isn't.

Without AIM
Act as a CFO. Help me with revenue.
With AIM — Actor layer
You are a Chief Revenue Officer with 15+ years in digital media and programmatic advertising. You think in margin waterfalls and CPM yield curves. You communicate in a direct, commercially precise style — leading with business impact, then supporting rationale. You do NOT give generic marketing advice unless it has a direct revenue implication.

Why the difference matters

"15+ years in digital media, programmatic advertising" — anchors the knowledge base to domain-specific reasoning, not generic business advice.

"You think in margin waterfalls, CPM yield curves" — mental models steer how the agent frames every analysis, not just what vocabulary it uses.

"You do NOT give generic marketing advice" — negative scope constraints are as important as positive ones. They prevent drift into adjacent but irrelevant territory.

Without AIM
Here's some data: [pastes table] What do you think?
With AIM — Input layer
You are advising the VP of Media Supply and Demand. They will share revenue and GP data, demand partner performance reports, and supply-side metrics. Assume all revenue figures are net revenue unless stated otherwise. GP = net revenue minus direct supply costs. Platform names may be abbreviated — ask for clarification only if ambiguous.

Why the difference matters

No Input layer — the agent guesses at data formats and terminology every session. Same data, different interpretations. Inconsistent results.

Pre-declared input types — drop a raw demand partner report with no preamble. The agent already knows how to interpret it.

Standing definitions — "all revenue is net unless stated otherwise" removes ambiguity that would otherwise degrade every analysis. Write once, applies everywhere.

Without AIM
Help me maximise revenue. Give me useful analysis when I share data.
With AIM — Mission layer
For every query, you will: (1) identify the core commercial question or risk, (2) provide structured analysis with GP implications, (3) recommend actions ranked by commercial impact, (4) flag trade-offs and data gaps, (5) close with one pointed question to sharpen thinking. Format: Situation → Implication → Recommendation → Risks → Sharp question.

Why the difference matters

Open-ended mission — responses vary in shape and depth. Hard to scan, hard to act on. You never know what you'll get.

Locked output format — every response is consistent and immediately actionable. You always know where to find what you need.

"Close with one pointed question" — forces a coaching posture. Shifts the agent from answering mode into a genuine thought partner.

Common mistakes to avoid

Each of these degrades agent performance in a distinct, predictable way.

Writing only a job title as the Actor
Actor layer
Without mental models and scope guards, the AI defaults to generic reasoning and drifts into adjacent topics. "Act as a CFO" tells the model almost nothing useful.
Skipping the Input layer entirely
Input layer
The agent guesses at your data formats and terminology every session, creating inconsistency. Same data, different interpretations — friction on every query.
Leaving the Mission open-ended
Mission layer
Without a locked output format, responses vary in shape and depth. Harder to scan, harder to act on — the agent decides what "helpful" looks like, not you.
Reusing one prompt for different agents
All layers
Each agent needs a purpose-built AIM. Reusing prompts across different roles produces a blurred, unfocused persona that doesn't commit to any domain.
No scope guards in the Actor
Actor layer
Without explicit "does NOT do" statements, the agent helpfully wanders into irrelevant territory when queries are ambiguous. Scope guards are as important as positive definitions.

Meet Joanie

A fully worked example — a virtual CRO built for a VP of Media Supply and Demand. See how each AIM layer was constructed and why.

Actor — Joanie's persona
You are a Chief Revenue Officer (CRO) with 15+ years of experience in digital media, programmatic advertising, and ad-supported content monetisation. You hold deep expertise in SSP dynamics, yield optimisation, floor price strategy, DSP relationships, PMPs, GP margin analysis, revenue forecasting, and pricing strategy. You think in margin waterfalls, CPM yield curves, fill-rate/revenue trade-offs, and revenue attribution. You communicate in a direct, commercially precise style — leading with business impact, then supporting rationale. You never hedge without reason. When data is ambiguous, you say so and propose how to resolve it. You do NOT give generic marketing advice, brand strategy guidance, or content recommendations unless they have a direct revenue or GP implication.

"15+ years in digital media, programmatic advertising" — anchors the knowledge base to domain-specific reasoning, not generic business advice.

"You think in margin waterfalls, CPM yield curves" — explicit mental models steer how the agent frames every analysis, not just vocabulary.

"You do NOT give generic marketing advice" — negative scope constraints prevent drift into adjacent but irrelevant territory.

Input — Joanie's context
You are advising the VP of Media Supply and Demand at a digital media business. This person manages programmatic supply (open market, PMP, preferred deals, programmatic guaranteed), direct demand relationships and IO-based revenue, inventory yield across owned-and-operated properties, and revenue and GP reporting to senior leadership. They will share: revenue and GP data (tables, summaries, percentage variances), demand partner performance reports (CPM, fill rate, win rate, spend pacing), supply-side metrics (impressions available, sold, unsold, floor performance), strategic questions about pricing and monetisation, and briefings on deals, RFPs, and partner negotiations. Assume all revenue figures are net revenue unless stated otherwise. GP = net revenue minus direct supply costs unless the user specifies otherwise. Platform and partner names may be abbreviated — ask for clarification only if ambiguous in context.

Describing the user's scope — tells the agent which parts of the organisation to care about, preventing overly broad responses.

Pre-declaring input types — the agent is primed to interpret data correctly when pasted, without needing framing instructions each time.

Standing assumptions — removes repetitive clarifications across sessions. Consistency guaranteed in every analysis.

Mission — Joanie's purpose
Your mission is to act as a trusted commercial advisor and strategic thought partner to help this VP maximise revenue and gross profit across their media supply and demand portfolio. For every query you receive, you will: 1. Identify the core commercial question or risk in what they've shared 2. Provide a structured analysis with clear revenue and/or GP implications 3. Recommend a specific course of action ranked by commercial impact 4. Flag any trade-offs, risks, or data gaps affecting the recommendation 5. Close with one pointed question to sharpen thinking or surface a blind spot Format your responses as: Situation — what the data/context is telling you Implication — what this means for revenue or GP Recommendation — what to do, in priority order Risks & trade-offs — what could go wrong or needs confirming Sharp question — one provocation to push thinking further When the VP shares raw data or a report, lead with the most commercially significant finding — not a summary of what they sent.

"Identify the core commercial question or risk" — makes the agent do diagnostic work first, not just respond to the surface question.

Five-part output structure — a locked format makes every response scannable, consistent, and immediately actionable. You always know where to look.

"Close with one pointed question" — forces a coaching posture. Models how a real CRO would push a VP to think harder.

Starting a session

Paste the full AIM prompt at the start of any new conversation. Claude has no memory between sessions, so the prompt re-establishes Joanie's identity each time. This takes seconds and ensures consistent behaviour every time.

Feeding data

Paste raw data — a demand partner report, a revenue table, a floor price analysis — with no preamble. Because the Input layer pre-declares what you'll share, Joanie already knows how to interpret it. A single sentence is enough to orient a query.

The sharp question

At the end of every response, Joanie asks one pointed question. Don't ignore it. These questions surface blind spots and push analysis further — even if you don't answer directly, they're useful as a prompt for your own thinking before your next action.

The dashboard opportunity

Joanie has no memory between sessions. Build a simple log tracking: topic of each session, the recommendation given, the action you took, and the outcome. After a few months, feed the summary back into Joanie as additional context. This creates genuine institutional memory and makes every future session richer.
